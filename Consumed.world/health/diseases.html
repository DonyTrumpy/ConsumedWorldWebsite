Diabetes mellitus' last results

Let's start with diabetes!

As we can see, there is no clear trend of where the most deaths due to diabetes (type II) are located. What can be said is how strong the top 4 is; For Croatia, Cyprus, Czech Republic and Malta it is clear that it is becoming problematic, especially for men.


There is a significant correlation of 0.52 (mainly for women) between diabetes and never drinking heavily in the last year. Inversely there is also a -0.52 correlation between diabetes and people drinking heavily at least once per month.

This means that there are less deaths due to diabetes in countries that drink more heavily (Western- and Northern Europe). This is a good example of something being correlated but not being a cause; Both just tend to come in opposite environments.


With this in mind, we also see that diabetes type II deaths tend to be higher in smoking countries (Eastern Europe) with a 0.52 correlation.

This correlation is even stronger when smoking more than 20 cigarettes per day (0.57).

Diabetes long-term

Cyprus has been dealing with this issue in a better way than in the past, but it remains a slow progress.

What is the most terrifying, is how rapidly the yearly death toll for some countries rises; As we can see the death toll of both Malta and Croatia tripled in less than 10 years! This problem is especially important for Croatia because from what we can see, it seems like the growth of it is entering a parabolic curve, while it seems to stabilize for Malta.

Some better news is that the top countries (United Kingdom, Iceland, Finland and others) have been able to keep their death toll at a low point and stabilize it.

Cancer's last results

Eastern Europe seems to be the region struggling the most against cancer with Hungary, Croatia, and Slovakia at the top. Men seem to be more vulnerable. The best countries against cancer are Cyprus, Switzerland, Malta, and Finland.


As one would expect, deaths from diseases is inversely correlated with life expectancy at the age of 65. In the case of cancer I mention it because it has a -0.74 correlation;

This means that for countries struggling with getting higher life expectancies, cancer is one of the main problems.

Some other deaths due to other diseases tend to come in the same environment as cancer; This is the case for ischemic heart diseases in men (0.65), deaths due to alcohol (0.43), and chronic liver diseases in men (0.66).


There is some correlation (0.4) among smoking men and cancer deaths; This correlation is even stronger when smoking more than 20 cigarettes per day. Also correlated but not very significant is that cancer deaths tend to occur in countries where women have never drunk heavily in the last year (0.38).

Cancer's long-term results

There are multiple types of cancer; they can come under different forms, be located differently, and spread differently which makes this disease very complicated to treat. In addition to being one of the largest death causes, the big unknown surrounding it makes it even scarier.

The good news is that for all countries and genders, the trend seems to lead towards lesser deaths. Let’s hope this continues!!

Ischemic heart diseases' last results

As we can see, we clearly have a big four!

Lithuania, Hungary, Latvia, and Slovakia together with the rest of Eastern Europe seem to be the ones struggling the most against ischemic heart diseases.

Again, men are on top!

The best countries against this type of disease are all located in Western Europe with Portugal, France and the Netherlands leading the way.


Deaths due to ischemic heart diseases tend to be higher in countries in which people smoke very heavily (more than 20 cigarettesper day), especially among women, with a correlation of 0.55.


Also, those heart deaths are correlated to not eating its fruits and vegies (0.4). This correlation disappears and even becomes negative (although not significant or bearily) as people eat their fruits and vegies (-0.3).

Finally, women dying of ischemic heart diseases are strongly correlated to women dying of liver diseases (0.68).

Ischemic heart diseases' long-term results

Even if the numbers in Eastern Europe are intimidating, the number of deaths (per 100 000) is going down over time. Additionally, the countries in better shape have been able to stabilize their death toll at a very low rate.

Alcohol's last results

Again, Eastern Europe is leading the way but this time we have a clear champion: Slovenia.

Interestingly, the countries drinking very heavily on a frequent basis are not the ones showing more deaths due to alcohol.

Clearly, men win once again, while women’s the death toll (even if bigger in Eastern countries) seems to be relatively stable.


For men, drinking heavily (at least once per week) is correlated with having a higher life expectancy (0.31).

This is because Northern and Western countries, in which the living conditions tend to be higher, are according to the data pretty alcoholic.

Nevertheless, deaths due to alcohol are as one should expect correlated with binge drinking (0.5) and inversely for having never drank heavily in the past year (-0.6). These correlations are especially true for women.

Alcohol's long-term results

The male death toll seems to be pretty stable as well in the best countries, but some countries have been struggling in the past years such as Poland (almost doubled in less than 10 years) but especially Slovenia!

Slovenia’s male death toll due to alcohol has quadrupled during the 2010’s! Even if alcohol consumption is heavily promoted, it is as well in other countries such as Belgium or Germany that are both on the higher half of the list as well, but do not even come close to Slovenia’s performance.

Other factors are in play. From what I have read there are political and social imbalances in Slovenia, but I do not have enough proof for it nor know enough about the country to make clear statements.

Liver diseases' last results

Guess the region... Drumrolls… Eastern Europe once again with sadly Romania, Slovakia, and Hungary on the podium!

This is likely due to the general diet as we saw in other graphs or in the findings that those Eastern countries do not binge drink on a frequent basis nor have a high death toll due to alcohol. Generally Eastern countries have lower incomes and have a diet rich in carbohydrates, meat, cream, and sugar which might not be the best on the long term.

AGAIN, men are leading the deaths.


Countries in which people die due to chronic liver diseases tend to be smoking countries (0.45).

Also, countries more vulnerable to this kind of diseases tend to not eat their fruits and vegies (0.52). Inversely, eating some fruits and vegies is inversely correlated with chronic liver deaths (-0.33). These findings are mildly stronger among women.

Liver diseases' long-term results

Altogether, the trend leads towards lesser deaths or at least a stabilization in them.

Northern- and Western Europe are having less problems with dealing with liver diseases and have stabilized their death toll at a low rate.

Nervous diseases' last results

Here we can see something very interesting: Europe’s wealthiest and most advanced countries are the ones struggling with the most deaths per 100 000 due to nervous diseases.

Finland (clear winner), Iceland and the United Kingdom are having the most problems, while Romania, Poland and Bulgaria are not struggling as much.

Men seem to be more vulnerable, but the difference is not that big.


There is a good inverse correlation (-0.5), between dying because of a nervouse disease and living in a smoking country (all smoking levels).

As already observed, Northern and Western countries tend to drink, while Eastern countries are more prone to smoking. So, it only makes sense that, inversely, deaths due to nervous diseases tend to occur in drinking countries (0.37).

Nervous diseases' long-term results

The trend is pretty stable in Eastern countries while in Northern- and Western Europe the trend is clearly rising and might become an even more important issue in the future.

It is probably not due to the cold since as we know it can get very cold in Poland, Estonia, Latvia, etc.

There might be a higher awareness and better diagnosis towards neurological diseases, but it seems hard to believe that even if that is the case that the Eastern countries would catch up to these high death tolls. There might be a genetical aspect between the populations, or as seen in our findings those countries are also the ones consuming the most alcohol. Also, the countries with less nervous diseases tend to consume the least alcohol.

It is probably the effect of a mixture of different things and full environment. Unfortunately, I do not currently possess the understanding to be able to pinpoint exactly, with full confidence, what is going on.

Pneumonia's last results

Here, there is no clear trend location wise. We have Poland, Slovakia and Romania and the United Kingdom as strong leaders in the death toll, while Greece, Croatia and Finland are able to keep it limited.

Pneumonia's long-term results

Even if for the better countries the death rate seems to be declining over time, the inverse is happening for the struggling countries and this especially among men.

Important note:

The numbers displayed are from 2011 to 2019, so some values probably have changed in the last 3 years. I am waiting for the “new” results of Eurostat and the OECD to update the data.
