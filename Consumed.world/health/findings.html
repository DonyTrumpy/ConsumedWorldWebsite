We've got correlations!

High correlations

Is the liver men's greatest weakness?

For men (at the population level), having a high life expectancy at 65 is inversely correlated with cancer, even more with cancer of the liver (-0.74), and diseases of the liver (-0.64).




Funny enough, countries with a high life expectancy at 65 are also countries with a high prevalence of circulatory diseases (0.6).

Eating more fruits and vegetables is correlated with a healthier liver (0.52).

Some diseases tend to come in similar environments.

For men, cancer is positively correlated with heart diseases (0.65) and liver diseases (0.66).

For women, heart diseases are positively correlated with liver diseases (0.68).

Choose your poison:

Populations with a higher percentage of heavy smokers have a 0.65 correlation with never drinking.

Inversely, populations with a lower percentage of heavy smokers have a -0.58 correlation with drinking heavily on a frequent basis.

Small correlations

Drinking heavily cures diabetes?

Populations in which people drink heavily on a more frequent basis tend to show less cases of deaths because of diabetes (-0.52).

This does not consider the number of people with diabetes, only the deaths. It might not actually be that heavy drinking cures diabetes, but rather a better healthcare system or other factors.

Again, it is in less developed countries that we find the most deaths due to diabetes and the most smoking populations (0.57), while as far as I know there is more sugar in a beer than in a cigarette. These findings point to an environment rather than a cause and effect.

Honorable mentions






Being in good health is inversely correlated with the level of air pollution (-0.45).

Cancer and drinking heavily on a frequent basis are correlated by 0.43.

Smoking populations are inversely correlated with eating fruits and vegetables (-0.4).
