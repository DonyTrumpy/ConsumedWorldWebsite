Where can we grow old and relax on our front porch?


As we can see here below, Northern countries have the longest life expectancy followed by Western countries. At last position we can see the Eastern European countries.

Even if women tend to live longer, the difference remains very slim.


As mentioned on other places of this website, deaths due to cancer is the strongest factor found against having a high life expectancy at the age of 65; Those two have a -0.74 correlation.

Life expectancy also has a -0.64 correlation with deaths due to chronic liver diseases. Funny enough it also has a positive (0.6) correlation with deaths due to ischemic heart diseases as well as another positive correlation (0.31) with drinking heavily at least once a week.

This is a friendly reminder that although things can be correlated, it does not mean one is the cause of the other.

They simply tend to come in the same environment and give us hints for where to look, what kind of questions to ask and where research might be needed.


Life expectancy is also correlated with eating its fruits and vegies; We can see that, on a population scale, not eating its fruits and vegies has a -0.5 correlation against life expectancy and inversely eating them has a 0.6 correlation.

Let's break the limits!

Good news! The worst countries are able to have a stable life expectancy and the better ones are seeing a nice little rise over time!

With this said, higher life expectancy does not say anyhting about the quality of life, even if it is probably assumed to go with it.


As of today, I am waiting very curiously for the 2020 results to see if the pandemic affected the scores.

Share of people in good health

Again, Western- and Northern-Europe have a higher proportion of their population than Eastern countries. Ireland, Switzerland and Greece are leading the way. While Portugal, Latvia and Lithuania are having a more difficult time.

There are plenty of factors needed for a healthy individual, but one that sometimes gets forgotten is air pollution;

Air pollution has a -0.45 correlation against the share of people in countries being in good health; Meaning that the more polution there is, the less healthy individuals tend to be.

A world of difference!

As we can see on the graph below, the share of people being in good health remains stable in the better countries and increases slightly year after year in the last European countries.


Even if it is good news that the trend tends to go up, the difference between the top and bottom countries remains stunning! The top countries have almost double the share of people being in good health.

Sadly, the bottom countries do not have yet 50% of their population being in good health. Inversely, the top countries have almost 85% of their population in good health.
